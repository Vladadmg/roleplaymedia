Permalink Reply by John H 1 day ago
Oh my. It was almost revolutionary when it came out. Let's see what I can remember.

Characters in Over The Edge (OTE) are defined by 3 positive traits (one of which is the primary trait), and a flaw. They should also decide on the character's motivation, a person who is important to the character, and a dark secret. The traits are rated from 1d6 to 4d6, and there should be something in the way the character looks or acts that shows the trait. Character traits that are very specialised, such as brain surgeon, are rated 1d6. Other traits are rated 3d6. One of your traits is superior, and gets an extra d6. Hit points are derived from the best trait which covers physical stuff.

Here's a sample character:

Description: An Orangutang Spirit who is permanently in human form.
Motivation: Collect interesting stuff, especially books.
Secret: Is an Orangutang Spirit.
Important Person: The “ancient wise one” who lives in the jungles of Africa.
Appearance: Fairly normal human, 35 years old, well-muscled, bright orange hair, arms seem a little too long.
Traits:
Orangutang Spirit  (primary, 4d6): Can swing through trees, eat copious quanities of bananas, excellent at climbing, very strong (bright orange hair, arms seem a little too long, scratches self a lot).
Knowledge Junkie  (1d6): Knows lots of useless facts, always wins at Trivial Pursuit, can always find information in libraries (quotes useless facts).
Locksmith  (1d6): Can pick most locks (carries lockpicks).
Flaw: Uncontrollable rage triggered by cruelty to animals, and also by people referring to animals using incorrect species (punches anyone who calls an ape a monkey)
Hit points: 28

Conflicts are resolved by comparing the total rolled by each side. Specialist traits are harder to beat than general traits, but I can't remember exactly how the rules work for that. I do remember that the rules that players need to know fit easily onto a single side of paper. The rules the GM needs to know are about six more pages. The rest of the book is setting: a crazy, whacked-out setting in which everything you read in newspaper tabloids might well true. And the centre of the wierdness is an obscure independant island state, which is the focus of the setting.

At the time it came out, my game group was playing relatively complex RPGs such as AD&D 2nd ed and Rolemaster, and we were firm believers that more realistic rules were better. OTE was a breath of fresh air for both its simplicity, and the fact that it made us realise that it was more important for the rules to really support the premise of the game than for them to be realistic. In this sense, OTE can be seems as one of the forerunners of the indie RPG movement. And the setting is simply fantastic.

There is more information over at Wikipedia and Geek Do. If you ever get a chance to play OTE, grab it!

Permalink Reply by Ryan Shelton on October 26, 2009 at 8:08am
I love--love--love--Beat to Quarters. I got to playtest it (most of the rules are ported over from Duty and Honour) and had a blast.

Full Disclosure: I am an Age of Sail nut. Especially for Napoleonic Naval stuff, so I am exactly the person Neil had in mind for this game.

Anyway, if you're interested, check out Omnihedron Games.

Also, in the very near future, RPGHaven Podcast will feature an interview with Neil Gow aka Omnihedron Games.

Duty and Honour (D&H) is about Wellington's Army in the Peninsular Campaign. You play soldiers in Portugal and Spain fighting against the Corsican Tyrant.

Beat to Quarters (BtQ) is about the Royal Navy during the Napoleonic Wars. You play sailors on the high seas fighting against the Corsican Tyrant.

One is not required to play the other. Although they share the same mechanics, each has specific tweaks for the settings. D&H has rules for skirmishes and larger land battles. BtQ has rules for ship engagements.

In either game you build a character by choosing your background, rank, and profession to get certain measures (attributes) and skills. Then you draw cards and compare them to some tables to determine your backstory; this provides you with additional measures and skills, as well as reputations (individual and institutional). Finally you pick Traits, perks that grant you bonuses to certain actions. Each game tweaks the skills, background tables, and traits for its setting.


When you want make a Challenge you draw a number of cards based on your relavent skill, reputations, and traits. The GM draws cards for your opposition based on the same. Then he draws a single card, called the Card of Fate. You are trying to match Number and Suit to the Card of Fate. A perfect Match is a perfect Success. A number match is a critical success, a suit match is a success. Whoever achieves the highest level of success, wins the test. Damage is done based on number and types of success to both parties. Note that the GM can never achieve a perfect success. Things are slightly stacked in the PCs favor.

Most often the Challenges are conflict resolution.

In a skirmish (which may be an actual military engagement or some other kind of activity in which all the players participate) The Commanding Officer gives bonus cards based on his Discipline Measure. Each character makes a test appropriate to the conflict (a marine might fire from the tops, the Gunner's mate would point the guns, a surgeon would patch wounds, the quartermaster would steer the ship) Each of these that succeeds gives the CO a bonus card on the final test. Each failure subtracts a card. The CO then makes a final test to determine the ultimate success or failure of the conflict.

Game play is broken down into missions. Each mission has a set number of challenges needed to complete the mission. A mission also has predetermined rewards (increases to skills and measures). Players can create personal missions (woo Lady Haverty, suck up to the First Lieutenant) and the GM creates military missions.

The game text explicitly advises player input for military mission creation.

There are a couple of specific things I love about D&H/BtQ.

* Chargen results in back-story that is specific but not detailed. (For example you might get the result "Served in the lead ships in a notable fleet victory. Gain X, Y, Z.")
* The rules are, in my opinion, very well thought-out and integrated. (For example, at first blush it might balk some players to think that the entire success of a Challenge is determined by the CO's roll. But it makes sense in the genre. Very capable subordinates can greatly enhance the CO's chances, and vise-versa.)

Having said all of that, this game might not be for you if:

* 1) You strongly prefer 'traditional' RPG paradigms. While there are simple tests, for say, breaking down a door, most of the resolution is on the confilct level.
* 2) You have little or no interest in a straight historical game. There's no rules for magic, miracles, or dragons (There is some talk of Neil make an accessory to cover this kind of thing).
* 3) Your group is not good at playing in a hierarchical social order. It's a military game. Someone needs to the be the Lieutenant and someone needs to the be a foremast jack. This is not to say that player's don't have equal input on the story. In fact the PCs all have something to do in a given conflict, but someone's PC will be issuing orders and someone's PC will be following them.

For the longest time it was the James Bond: 007 system from the 1980s, and it's still one of my favorites for espionage and modern action. Lately, I'd have to say it's a tie between Cortex ( Serenity & Battlestar Galactica ) and Hollow Earth Expedition .

My favorite game that I actually get to play is Star Wars: Saga Edition; it gets ride of a lot of the stuff that used to bog down my D&D 3.x game (iterative attacks, convoluted trip/grapple mechanics, spells) and retains enough traditional d20 crunch (feats, skills, talents) for my players.
