Choose independent designers / small press games?

  • Posted by T. A. Holaday on October 28, 2009 at 10:30am
  • View T. A. Holaday's blog
I like to think about the choices I make, why I make them, and how they compare to the choices and methods of choosing displayed by others. Yesterday I was looking at my collection of RPG's and trying to establish by looking at the history of my purchases where my tastes lie now, where they have been in the past, whether my purchases have been reflective of those tastes (am I satisfied with the things I bought), etc.

Noticing a preference for small press games, I asked myself what I found attractive about these games. Specifically, I wanted to know what I was getting by purchasing small press games, and conversely what I might be missing as a result of not choosing their counterparts.

I came up with two key elements that I like about small press games, which I want to share in a nonjudgmental way. Meaning I am going to write about what I personally think I am getting by purchasing small press games without saying "and you should purchase small press games too." Hopefully I will explain how each of these elements are a matter of choice and what the effects of the choices are, leaving other gamers better informed to make choices of their own.

COHESIVE VISION
More often than not, small press games are the product of one person's vision, or a collaboration between two or three designers. (Obviously larger press games sometimes, but less commonly, fit this description as well.) The advantage of having only one vision, or a vision closely shared by only a few individuals, is purity and potency. Purity in the sense that the vision isn't being stretched out of shape to include the various ideas or exclude the dislikes of a team of creative individuals. As such, pure visions are often more unique, which also gives them more impact and/or potency. The disadvantage to such a vision is that it may target a narrower audience and can be polarizing. Gamers will either love the game or hate it.

The advantages of the opposite type of game, one that is the creative output of a whole team of designers, is theoretically a more evenly developed and comprehensive product, and also one with more universal appeal. The drawback is that these products are also sometimes less artistically coherent, can be bland, and at worst represent a lowest common denominator. Instead of getting the best ideas from each team member and combining them, sometimes these games are a compromise representing an amalgamation of the bits to which the fewest number of team members objected.

ARTISAN MENTALITY
Small press books often display an artisan's mentality about product. There is something kind of magical about a small press book. Contact with a small press book often sparks a feeling of intimacy, because the books look and feel as if they were (or could have been) made by a person, rather than a printing press. Tell-tale signs of small press books are card stock covers, saddle stitching (which is a fancy word for binding pages together by stapling through the crease) or tape binding, unorthodox or even clunky layouts and fonts, less polished (but not necessarily poor) artwork, etc. All of these are cues that the creator is working without the same resources that a big company might have. While this means the book probably won't hold up as well to long term use, the trade-off is a sense of participation in something special. The buyer of a small press game is really more of a patron. Generally, the creator will recover a greater percentage of the profit and be paid more directly. The buyer of a small press book can assume he will have more direct access to the creator. The buyer can assume he is joining a smaller community, in which his or her voice may carry more weight than it would in the fan base of a large press game.

Sometimes small press books even bear the physical marks of their creators. I have known designers to hand-make covers using various arts-and-crafts techniques, to individually sign and/or number copies, to create alternative covers, etc. These books obviously appeal to the collector mentality that most gamers (and most human beings) have to a greater or lesser degree.

Large press books generally have the advantages of larger print runs, which can reduce the cost-per-page and/or justify higher quality printing processes. In general, large press books have a more polished feel to them and will last longer. However, just as small press books have observable features that mark them as having been produced on a budget, large press books always look and feel mass-produced. The buyer of a large press book trades off intimacy for a bigger fan base, which means he or she is more likely to be able to find other gamers with the same game. Also, buyers of large press books can assume that some quantity of supporting products are probably in the works or are already on the shelf. (This can be a bad assumption, but generally speaking it is accurate.)

OTHER
Of course these two elements aren't the only reason to choose a small or large press game, but they are certainly two important factors in my own purchasing decisions (aside from other factors that are unrelated to the small or large press format). If you find yourself being swayed by the same factors, even if you make the opposite choices I generally do, take a moment to tell me about it.

Comment by Scott Keenan on October 29, 2009 at 1:00am
I think for me, what draws me to small press games is that their creators have been free to experiment in whatever way they think is necessary. A large press publisher, on the other hand, is somewhat bound to produce a game that contains more tried-and-true elements (mechanics, setting, subject matter, writing style, art choice, authorial voice).

More experimentation, I figure, leads to more innovation. There are a lot of small press games out there and by no means do they all end up more innovative than large press, but the large number means that there will often be one out of the many small press games that offers something truly unique and new that advances the state of the design art.
