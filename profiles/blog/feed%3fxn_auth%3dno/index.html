One of the games I sold was my original Red Box Basic D&amp;D game and I regret that for two reasons - 1, it was a great little game and I get all teary-eyed and nostalgic sometimes for some classic gaming and 2, it was written in a style that eased new players into roleplaying games and didn't have them running for the hills because the rulebook seemed to be written in a different language and was so big you could beat someone to death with it.<br/><br/>And here lies my problem.<br/><br/>Rulebooks these days seem pretty impenetrable for the first-time or young gamer who's entering the hobby cold, that is without any kind of peer to help with explanations and examples. In fact, the whole hobby seems to be something of an enigma to those who don't know the game or haven't been involved with it for longer than a few years. I did a little digging by sticking the basics into a search engine, acting as if I was a new and interested gamer with no experience, and I was somewhat mystified by the results.<br/><br/>I got the basic details about roleplaying from Wikipedia - that much was sort of okay - and after sifting through computer and online roleplaying games I finally found some dedicated tabletop roleplaying websites. What I was greeted with was a whole mess of voices and opinions using words and phrases that, if I had been a new gamer, would have confused me straight away and possibly made me change my mind about getting into gaming, especially if I <span style="font-style: italic;">had</span> been a (lot) younger and a bit shy. Lots of different posts from people, some of them vitriolic and confrontational filled with words and references I would have had no idea about. I don't want to surf the internet looking for translations or meanings, I just want to play a game and if I have to be made to work, or even seemingly <span style="font-style: italic;">earn</span>, that privelige then what's the point? Gaming websites seem to cater to the experienced gamer and that will immediately put off new players as they don't want to seem like they're intruding and they don't want to look foolish.<br/><br/>Then there's the rulebooks. Some of them are somewhat accessible but otherwise they can be tomes of words that make no sense. They can be massive books filled with options and tables that have little meaning because the new gamer has no context. Sure, follow the rules as far as creating a character is concerned and learn the basics of dice-rolling, but how do you use it? The advice in some books can be cryptic and confusing as it doesn't talk about gaming in general but about gaming in that particular rulebook's world.<br/><br/>So, this is where I come back to basic D&amp;D. It spoke to the new gamer as a kid, because it was kids that got this hobby fired up in the first place. Pre-teen and teenage kids who wanted to pretend to be warriors, wizards and elves. The books took them through everything, held their hand and explained it all in simple, kid-like terms. That's how I learned the game, and the only thing that made me nervous was sitting down for those first few games with other people. I didn't have these swathes of web pages making my head spin with words and phrases I didn't understand, or hundreds of experienced gamers telling how I <span style="font-style: italic;">should</span> be playing a game of let's pretend.<br/><br/>I sometimes think that the RPG community is it's own worse enemy. They talk about the fading hobby but they spend so much time carping on about the minute details of their game using incomprehensible and sometimes offensive language that they don't realise that they're scaring away potential new gamers. We've managed to make the hobby this strange game in the shadows that people are afraid to approach because we simply <span style="font-style: italic;">make no sense</span> - this was bad back in the 1980s when I joined in but the internet has only served to make that worse. How can we compete with the popularity and simplicity of MMORPGs and CRPGs if this is the case? <br/><br/>So this is what I miss. Simple, talk-to-them-like-children roleplaying games that ease gamers into the hobby with simple rules and long-winded explanations. A game that stands on it's own, like the different coloured boxes of Basic D&amp;D.<br/><br/>Roleplaying games seem to be a dying breed and I have to wonder sometimes just who's fault that is.</p> <p> </p> <p><em>Originally posted at <a href="http://www.farsightblogger.blogspot.com">www.farsightblogger.blogspot.com</a></em></p> Nains des Sphères Connues tag:www.roleplaymedia.net,2011-02-12:4303603:BlogPost:12629 2011-02-12T08:31:59.000Z David http://www.roleplaymedia.net/profile/David <div style="text-align: justify;"><span style="font-family: trebuchet ms,geneva; color: #800000;"><strong><em>Dans les Sphères, les Nains n'ont encore jamais réussi à unifier leurs forces afin de forger une nation puissante. By comparison, <em>Raiders</em> provides specific information on the village and rather than a random encounter table, there are a number of specific encounters in <em>Raiders</em>.<br /> <br /> The failure to detail the village of Salt Marsh is the largest failure of <em>Sinister Secrets</em>, something which cannot simply be written off as a product of the time and place where the module was produced. The module devotes 536 words to telling the game master to design the actual village of Salt Marsh for themselves and provides an outline for doing so.<br /> <br /> Quoting from the module;<br /> <br /> “Therefore, if there is to be an adequate background to the series, the DM is recommended to 'prepare' the town quite thoroughly, using the guidelines presented in this module as the basis for that preparation.”<br /> <br /> This is utterly useless because the <em>Dungeon Master Guide</em> already provided a lot of this guidance and any DM could have done this on their own, on the fly if need be, without the step by step instructions and in addition the text has a sanctimonious tone. Hell, it is worse than useless – had the page been blank, then it would have given the buyer space to doodle out information on the village. In any event, why not spend those 530 some odd words describing the village rather than telling us how to do something we already know how to do?<br /> <br /> <em>Sinister Secrets</em> in and of itself has almost no story and is largely an elaborate site, which has connections to other locations. Perusing and investigating the connections can provide a story but it is optional. <em>Raiders</em> has a story, which is railroad in nature – that is to say the party get on board the plot train and is carried along, almost regardless of the decisions of the party members.<br /> <br /> <em>Sinister Secrets</em> provides monster stats and maps at the back or on the inside cover, where <em>Raiders</em> mostly provides this material close to the appropriate encounter. This is not absolute, as some of the maps and monsters stats in <em>Raiders</em> are also in the back, but it does show a shift to the 4E layout format, where the maps and monsters are all close to the appropriate encounter in the book, minimizing the amount of flipping back and forth required.<br /> <br /> <em>Sinister Secrets</em> also provides character guidance, such as stating lawful characters will automatically comply with taxes levied on the treasure the party amasses. The <em>Raiders</em> adventure does not do this at all, presumably because it and its writers trust the game master and players know this already.<br /> <br /> <em>Sinister Secrets</em> involves pirates and smugglers using a reputedly haunted house as a base of operations, only to have the stories of the haunting they spun as a cover get the attention of the party. Wackiness ensues. <em>Raiders</em> have a tribe of kobolds, suborned by a young dragon, attacking local settlements. Again, once the party gets involved, wackiness ensues.<br /> <br /> As the party investigates the situation in <em>Sinister Secrets</em>, they meet an assassin who is pretending to be prisoner of the villains. This part of the scenario, where an apparent victim is simply part of the trap for the PCs, became a cliché if not a hallmark of many of the early adventures in Dungeon magazine and the published modules. This assassin tells the party he is a second level thief. There is no way that could have gone down as anything other than an odd scene. Theoretically, the characters at least do not think of themselves in terms of levels, even assuming they think of themselves in terms of character class. It is like the scene in Kevin Smith’s movie, <em>Jay and Silent Boy Strike Back,</em> where the drug dealer characters run across other drug dealers and they all show each other their drug dealer union badges and bitch about going on strike. In the Smith movie, it is supposed to be an absurd scene but in <em>Sinister Secrets</em>, the scene is apparently supposed to be played straight.<br /> <br /> Once they leave the village in <em>Raiders</em>, aside from some gnome prisoners, the party does not encounter anyone they are not supposed to kill and they know it. There are no transparent traps the players must toss their characters into for the sake of plot.<br /> <br /> The tacit racism of D&amp;D and RGPS in general, where entire races are inherently evil and exist to die at the hands of the player characters, is the subject of another column. For good or ill, <em>Raiders</em> stays true to this mindset in terms of the kobolds and hobgoblins, even if it gives names to many of the kobolds, which is a bit like giving names to the individual chips in a bag of Doritos. The villains of <em>Sinister Secrets</em> are pirates and smugglers, humans who chose to be evil during a job placement service and as such, can be killed with a reckless abandoned usually reserved for… well, kobolds and hobgoblins.<br /> <br /> So, then what are the structural and thematic differences between the first edition and old school module and the fourth edition and new school module?<br /> <br /> Aside from the failure of the module <em>Sinister Secrets</em> to provide anything remotely useful on the village of Salt Marsh, it is a solid adventure, requiring a decent level of work and dedication on the part of the adventurers and offering plenty of chances for combat. It has a gradual build until the party finds themselves, almost by accident, in the villains lair and surrounded by people who want to stab them repeatedly in the head.<br /> <br /> <em>Raiders</em> more quickly plunges the party into the action, with combat between the adventures and the raiding kobolds occurring quickly. The party still has to be persistent, as patting themselves on the back after the hostage rescue is premature and an invitation to additional attacks from the monsters. The party must to chase down the villains in their lair to properly complete the mission.<br /> <br /> To ascribed genres to the modules, <em>Sinister Secrets</em> is a thriller and Raider is a straight up action adventure. Understanding that lets the reader know what they are getting and better select the module they want.<br /> <br /> In terms of structure, <em>Sinister Secrets</em> suffers from being a product of its day. The presentation of the information is simply better in contemporary material than it was in the years surrounding the appearance of the game. The loose cover meant the maps were always close at hand, even if they were often functional rather than attractive or plausible. However, the original books usually had a layout and composition requiring a lot of flipping back and fourth through the module and the three core books. In terms of composition, this is not the issue in 4E it was in first edition, thought it will never entirely go away. In 4E as much as possible information relevant to an encounter is presented at the encounter itself. Because the game is less about rolling dice and mathematics than is it about fake battle with imaginary monsters, quickly accessible game mechanics which halve the table time required to get through a fight is a good thing. Mostly, the time cut otherwise involves flipping through books to find the right map and bickering over the page number of where to find the description of a particular power.<br /> <br /> So what can these two modules tells us about old school versus new school gaming?<br /> <br /> Surprisingly, relatively little. The biggest difference between the two modules largely comes down to ones of tone, in which case it is counter productive to compare the two – by analogy it is not very useful to compare the films Fallen and Rush Hour. Structurally, in terms of the presentation of the material, <em>Raiders</em> and most 4E products are superior to first edition modules and books. It is simply easier to find the right information.<br /> <br /> The Urban Dictionary define “old school” as;<br /> <br /> “…games that tested players' wits, could kill off careless characters, and required dedication and inner strength to play. Old school games didn't pander to the ideas that everyone is created equal, that all options are open to all races, that the markets were somehow free, and that a quasi-medieval society could have near 100% literacy.”<br /> <br /> Both adventures present chances for the characters to die if they are sloppy and if the DM has gotten a sour result on his daily pity roll. Dead because a smuggler stabbed your character in the back of their head or dead because kobolds pushed a spear through your characters face is still dead. Both modules require dedication to pursue the adventure to the end, to find and defeat all the villains. There is nothing in either adventure to suggest all the NPCs, or PCs for that matter, are somehow equal, literate and liberal in some vaguely defined Southern Californian way. Being civil to NPCs is useful in <em>Raiders</em>, but it should be in <em>Sinister Secrets</em> as well, even if the actual village remains undefined. Both <em>Sinister Secrets</em> and <em>Raiders</em> also offer opportunities for going through a dungeon, killing everything killable and looting all the corpses.<br /> <br /> As I have observed before, the rules of the game – regardless of the edition – are not the laws of physics and only function to the extent they are made to function by the players and the game master. The edition does not actually determine if the people at the table play loose with the rules, or stringently adhere to the rules as written.<br /> <br /> Which brings us back to the question that framed this discussion, namely what are the differences between old school and new school? These two modules, one written for first edition, one written for fourth edition, are both designed for low level characters and are both composed by people outside the of main D&amp;D offices.<br /> <br /> A good game master can recreate the thriller-like tone of <em>Sinister Secrets</em>, should they so desire, even if its slow burn quality has always set it apart. <em>Raiders</em> is standard action adventure fair. It is unlikely anyone seriously misses the weak formatting which runs through the oldest game books. Aside from that, the differences come down to execution. If that is the case then new school and old school gaming are a matter of perspective and the terms are so vague as to be useless. What I mean is, it's not a messy sheet to work on. I like to work on my games, make them unique and my own. Many games either give me a solid, ready game to pick up and play, which by the way there's nothing wrong with I like a lot of games like that, or give me just the absolute bare minimum, not giving me such things as basic settings or, say, skills.<br/>When 4th edition came out it had three books; the player's handbook, the dungeon master's guide and the monster manual.
